Automotive applications often use bevel gears to change the direction of power between the engine and the rear wheels. The smaller drive gear is again called the pinion, and the larger driven gear is called the ring gear or the crown gear.

Hypoid bevel gears have teeth pitched on a hyperbolic—pinched cylinder—surface. While spur gears have shafts in the same plane (axis), bevel gears allow the shafts to be on different planes. The different axis allows the pinion's contact surface to be larger and provide more torque. Rear-wheel-drive vehicles frequently use hypoid bevel gears.
