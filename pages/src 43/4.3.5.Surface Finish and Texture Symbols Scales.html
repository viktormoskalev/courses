The table lists the standard finish values in microinches, micrometers, and N-scale and some applications and methods of producing the finish. Several methods will yield a given finish, so the machinist must decide which one to use to produce the desired quality with the least expense. For instance, a finish of 32 microinches is possible on the mill. However, this finish is near the limit of the capability of many milling operations, but it’s well within limits for grinding.

The machinist may mill the part roughly to size and then finish by grinding to get both the dimensional tolerance and the finish required. Another consideration is the heat treat specification. If the part needs heat treating after milling and needs to be finish-ground, it makes no sense to spend the extra time milling the part to a good finish, only to grind this finish away in the final operation.

Figure 6 lists many of the common surface texture symbols and the meanings and placement of values. Some of the notes indicate that the surface must be machined, regardless of the raw stock's surface finish. Other notes indicate that the surface shouldn’t be machined and that the given surface finish is an inspection tool. The surface finish symbol can also include directions on how a part should be machined by specifying the direction—the lay—of the tooling marks.

Finally, the surface finish symbol can include a value for the amount of material left on before finishing a particular surface. As we discussed earlier, the amount of material allowance may also be listed in a note or might be left up to the machinist to decide.

However, the geometric tolerances and surface finish symbols require the machinist to set up and machine it in a particular order. Note that the bottom face of the part is datum A, and the larger diameter end of the casting is datum B. Here’s the order of operation for this cast piece:

  1. 1

    Check thickness of the part.

  2. 2

    Set up the part, with the bottom (datum A) facing up.

  3. 3

    Locate the part off the large outer diameter on the left side of the part.

  4. 4

    Machine datum A face, taking off only enough material to clean up the surface, making sure there’s sufficient material left on to clean up the opposite side when bringing the 1.500" dimension into tolerance.

  5. 5

    In the same setup, machine the two bores.

  6. 6

    Flip the part, and machine the other face to the 1.500" dimension.
