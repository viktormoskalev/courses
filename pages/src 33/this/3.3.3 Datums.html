The primary datum plane is the initial starting point for all subsequent measurements on the part. It’s established by three points of contact, which can be identified by datum target symbols on a drawing. High school geometry teaches us that two points define a line, and three points define a plane. Three points can be chosen from the real part and used to define

the perfect plane for measurement purposes. Even the primary datum feature can have variations, and the tolerance assigned to this surface measured to the perfect datum plane. Figure 32 shows a simple object with two datum planes and the symbols used to identify the datums.

Using a perfect datum plane perpendicular to the primary datum plane, you can establish a secondary datum plane.

The third—tertiary—data plane is then perpendicular to both the primary and secondary planes. Figure 33 shows the relationship between the three datum planes, usually labeled A, B, and C in drawings. Identifying the primary, secondary, and other datums are important for both machining and inspection since the variability of features can

affect whether a part is acceptable or not. If a part is machined using one surface as a datum and then inspected using another as the primary datum, it might cause the rejection of a good part or acceptance of a bad part. Figure 34—with exaggeration of features—shows how this could occur on a part without datums specified. If you read prints as part of a machining or inspection job, make sure you know how to interpret the datums and features in relation to each other.
