In designing gear teeth, the shape of the tooth must have precise dimensions and curvature to make sure it meshes properly with other gear teeth—called an involute curve. This curve is the shape you would trace with a pencil tied to a piece of string as it unwinds from the surface of a cylinder. The specific form is necessary, so the teeth make contact at the same distance from the center of each gear at every point during the rotation—from the first contact

of each tooth until it disengages. Since the surfaces are curved, as a pair of gears turn, the two gear teeth will always contact an angle—called the pressure angle , as shown in Figure 21. Standard gear forms use either a 20° or 14 1/4° contact angle, with the 20-degree angle being more popular in modern manufacturing designs.

Another important dimension on gears is the pitch diameter. The pitch diameter is an imaginary circle whose diameter is at the approximate midpoint of the tooth height, as shown in Figure 22. All gears that mate properly will have their pitch diameters tangent to one another, as shown in Figure 23.

The gear width determines how much horsepower it can transmit since its full tooth width transmits the gear's force. The face width of the gear is the thickness of the tooth along the axis of the gear. Drafters often put important gear data in a table on the print, as shown in Figure 24. Helical gears—with teeth cut at an angle to the face—are similar to spur gears. An example appears in Figure 26. Helical gears can provide higher torque, last longer, and run quieter and smoother at high speeds. A common application is in a transmission. Some uncommon, special application helical gears have shafts cut that aren’t parallel.
