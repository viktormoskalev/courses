The machinist can read the dimension from the machine's digital readout—vernier scale during production. The tolerance allows for a location variance on both sides of the drilled hole, making it easier to match readings on the machine with dimensions on the print.

It reduces errors and allows quicker manufacturing by using the entire tolerance on the dimension.

If the part had only one stated tolerance, a variation in the wrong direction would create a useless piece. The shown bilateral tolerance has equal positive and negative tolerances. However, both tolerances don’t need to be equal—allowing an uncentered theoretical location. For example, a dimension of 2.50 +0. 010 / –0.005 shows that the engineer wants a 2.50 dimension, but the machinist can place the feature anywhere between 2.510 and 2.495 and still be within tolerance.

When setting up a part with unilateral or bilateral tolerances that aren’t equal, the machinist should usually set up to machine the part to a dimension centered between the two limits. This setting allows the machinist the maximum use of the tolerance and allows the most variation during production while remaining within the required tolerance.

Sometimes, a machinist will set up to machine a part to its maximum material condition (MMC).

They might do this when the part is difficult and expensive to produce. Machining to the MCC will allow the most material to remain after tooling. For example, they might drill a hole towards the lower tolerance since they can rework the piece, if necessary, to remove additional material but can't add material back onto the piece. The decision to stay on the MMC-side of tolerance comes after considering factors of cost, time, material availability, and the part's functionality.
